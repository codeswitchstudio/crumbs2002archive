Uncle Wowie

The place would smell faintly of shit when he was around. Liquid shit. That was about two or three months before he was confined to Philippine General. Two or three months before he died.

When he was younger, he'd drink gin and whiskey and beer with his cousin Lawrence in my grandmother's house. I'd be trying to sleep in the living room couch, while Jim Morrison and grass and the sound of beer-filled glasses hitting one another trail their way to my mind, from outside. When drunk (but not drunk enough), he'd wake me up and ask me to accompany him to the neighborhood store to buy more beer. We'd walk around Kalantiaw at midnight, chasing cats and kicking dogs as we went from one closed store to the next. After drinking, he'd shout and rant in his sleep, raving like a lunatic: "You wanna see my cock... ? C'mere, baby, I'll... I'll show you my... cock... cockity-cock-cock..."

He used to tease me about how my right hand would twitch and jerk when I listened to my records, mock-strumming at an air guitar. "Why do you do that?" he asked me one time, as I came out of the bathroom. I brought the player inside the bathroom with me, listening to Pearl Jam's VITALOGY (my hand riffing along with 'Corduroy' that moment). I looked down on my right hand and caught it jerking here and there, in synch with Stone Gossard in the speakers. I smiled awkwardly, a bit ashamed (maybe even blushing a bit). I just shrugged my shoulders and walked (actually, sulked in shame) towards my makeshift-room in the sala.

The week after that, he had his belly against an operating table, getting his intestines cut off of him, (through his ass, where they made a wide slice across his butt-crack, which they stitched together right after). He was diagnosed with cancer of the colon ("cans-arse" is what I call it), in its advanced stage already. I remember him telling me his intestines were riddled with yellow tumors, already. Beyond hope. They had to take out his intestines, stitch a plastic tube to a plastic ring-lined hole about an inch or so from his navel, leading from his stomach to a Ziplock-caliber plastic bag hanging by his belly, where he was supposed to shit in.

He was always standing up when commuting, as he couldn't sit on his ass, as it was sore as hell (even when the jeep was empty, he'd still be hanging out its entrance-exit, by the handle bar). In my grandmother's house, he cleaned his bag in the bathroom sink, with Pride laundry soap. Sometimes Tide. He would do it in the afternoon, as there were less people in the house in the afternoon. Less people using the bathroom. I stayed there in the afternoons (it was summer vacation), playing Wolfenstein 3D in my aunt's 386. After washing, he would go to the room and talk to me while I played. And we would talk, and he would talk, and I would listen. And the whole time, he'd smell of shit. Not a disgusting shit-smell, actually. It was a clean smell. Hospital smell. But I knew it was shit.

During our afternoon talks, I realized several things: he was young (he was thirty-three back then, the youngest in their family) and alive, so much still in front of him, for the taking, but he felt tired and old and aching all in the wrong parts (his heart, his mind, his belly and his arse). He was knocked-down, burned-out, stank like medicated shit and limp like a glove. He had an estranged wife that wouldn't make love with him, a young son that he saw two days a week, and a plastic bag for an ass.

"Look at me," he said one Thursday afternoon (the sky outside was gray, yellow lamp-light bathing his handsome features as he carefully sat up on bed). It was our last heart-to-heart talk. "Look at me. I'm a monster, man. I shit in a bag, I can't eat pizza, I can't even ride a jeepney properly. You know I have to stand and hang by the handle bars, even if it's fucking empty?"

He paused for a while, looking at himself in the mirror screwed to the wall opposite the bed. After a minute or two, he said: "I can't live like this. You know what keeps me going? You know the driving force that keeps me alive in mornings? It's Joachim." Joachim is his son's name. "And I don't want him to see me like this. But... but."

He stopped, all of a sudden. He slowly crawled towards me (I was sitting on a chair beside the bed, looking at him), held my hand, looked at my eyes. "Make me a promise. A promise to a dying man." Sounds a bit weird, I know. It's one of those lines of dialogue you only encounter under extremely weird occurrences.

"A promise to a dying man." he said. I nodded. He told me, almost in a whisper, so no one would hear, not even God or the angels. For my ears only.

He told me, and I agreed. We sealed the promise with a handshake.

Three days later, he was confined to Philippine General. My mother and I visited him on his second day there. A nurse was washing the wound on his ass-crack, which was now infected like hell. I imagine it looked like pizza. His belly was unnaturally swollen with gas. The tube that led from his stomach to the belly-hole was inflamed. He was constipated. He was in so much pain, all he could do was whine and wheeze under his breath, the sounds a bit raspy already. A first thing for me: I saw Pain in his eyes. His mouth was open, his jaws slacked a bit. His skin was pale and clammy to the touch. Cold sweat. And his eyes. In his eyes was Pain.

He died two days later. Joachim wasn't even able to see him, as children below twelve weren't allowed in the ICU rooms. He was cremated three days later in Guadalupe. I refused to go to the funeral. Those who attended drank gin and whiskey and beer, and played Jim Morrison in the closed-circuit audio system.

I spent the day in my room, listening to 'the Unknown Soldier'.

In one of my bookshelves, there is a picture of me during the opening of our high school graduation, seated between Mark Louie DeGuzman and Andrew Cementina. We did the ceremonies in Abelardo Hall at UP (was a running joke the whole day: "Where did you graduate?" "Me? I graduated in UP!!"). My hair still short from CAT, but slowly creeping down towards my jawline were my sideburns, like soft downy female pubes.

In the picture (actually, even in the flesh), on several angles, I look a lot like my uncle.

- adam
