It was THE WEEPING DEMON when the doorbell rang.

With about thirty "I'm sorry" messages in the answerphone, I wasn't expecting anyone coming to my birthday. Still in my underwear, I walk to the bed to put on my pants, shouting Hold on! over my shoulder. I open the door.

"What took you so long to answer the door?" It's Saffron, soaking with rainwater.

I was in my underwear, I tell her. I had to wear a pair.

I let her in, helping her remove her brown corduroy jacket. I remember buying this for her, way back, as a token of appreciation. Bought it from this bargain place beside an army base. Saffron and I were there, doing research for this photos collection book we were doing. It was her contact, and I was looking for a job. Asked me if I wanted one for the summer. I was just starting then. Said yes, and got paid up front, one of my first cheques. Was happy, felt immensely indebted to her.

We saw the bargain place when we were looking for a nice spot to shoot these army jeeps (black against gray). Like a satellite to a planet, she gravitated towards the bargain bins automatically, like they were soulmates. Digging into the bins and boxes full of clothes, she pulled out this beaten-up brown corduroy jacket. She put it on and did a little twirl (like a ballerina), a smile of bliss plastered on her face. I smiled and walked towards the shopkeeper. How much for that jacket? I asked her. "One hundred fifty, ho" she answered me. I pulled out three fifty peso bills, crisp like dead leaves, and I paid for the jacket.

"As if I haven't seen you in your drawers before!" she tells me as I go to the laundry room with the dripping jacket. She follows me, her clothes drip-drip-dripping water on the floor. Take those things off, I tell her, pointing at her shirt and pants. You'll die of pneumonia if you're not careful. "Yeah, yeah" she mumbles, taking off her shirt. I walk to the closet, got my KISS ME shirt and a pair of pajama bottoms I haven't used for years. Trying not to look, I hand it to her. Here, wear these things instead, I say. Shove your things in the dryer and turn it on.
