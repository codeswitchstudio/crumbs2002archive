"It was fun, you know?" she thought out aloud. We were both in bed, on our sides, facing each other, the radio singing 'Diamond Sea' in Thurston Moore's voice. Her things were packed and tucked under the bed a few hours earlier. I even helped her pack, somewhat half-hearted. What was fun? I asked her. She smiled her dimpled smile at me before answering. "Our time together, dummy. It was fun. I had fun," she said, and she leaned towards me and gave me a kiss.
And like that, she stood there in front of me. I raised the camera to my right eye and took aim. Click, the Zenith went. I pulled the photograph out and waved it to dry. "Not bad," Azalea told me when the picture was clear. And then she gave me a kiss, and after that, we made love one final time, the act still as gentle and sweet and magical as our first time together.

For every kiss planted came a rain of quick pecks.

I fell asleep, and when I woke up three hours later, she was gone.

I reached out for the table behind me and felt for a pack of Camels and a blue Cricket lighter. A tap and a light, and I was smoking a stick between my lips. As I turned back to face Azalea, I found her naked and on her feet by the window, the bedsheets draped on her shoulders, the Zenith in hand, facing me. "Smile, Siddharta," she told me in a whisper, and then took my picture. Something caught my eye just then.

I sat up on the bed and took the camera from her. Just stand there, I told her. I have the best shot, here.

I slipped the Polaroids back into the pages of the book, and I slipped the book into the stack behind me, between a book about Origami and my hardcover copy of 'the Elephant Vanishes'.

Days later, Saffron was shuffling through the Kama Sutra when she found the Polaroids. "Who's this girl?" she asked me, waving Azalea's picture in the air. That? I asked her. She nodded.

A girl I knew once, back in college… I told her, leaving it hanging.

"Does she have a name?" she asks me after a second or two. I nodded, saying Yeah, she does. "What's her name, then?"

Azalea, I told Saffron. She smiled and nodded, as if she understood, as if enlightened.

"Az-za-le-ah," Saffron repeated the name, letting the name linger on her lips like sweet cream soda aftertaste.

She slipped the Polaroids back into the book, and placed the book back into the stack. She looked back at me and smiled.
