I met Azalea in college. She was an exchange student from Singapore, studying Anthropology. She was one of this group of 10 students from across South-East Asia who were brought in as an International Friendship Gesture of sorts: the rest of South-East Asia welcomed Filipinos, and in turn, we welcomed our Foreign Neighbors with open arms. In hindsight, I think that the source of my attraction to Azalea was her being foreign. Like with Gerda, Azalea was like a whole new country for me. She was Erika and Miss Min-Mei and Cio-Cio San.

She was sushi and wasabe and sake. She was Japan.

"Sid-dhar-ta," she read my name aloud, first time we met, pronouncing each syllable not too fast, not too slow, my name dancing on her lips. Perfectly, in that wonderful English accent of hers, classy and refined. Az-za-le-ah, I read her name in turn, letting it linger like sweet cream soda aftertaste. She smiled and I smiled, and for some reason we both laughed at our mutual fondness of each other's names.

The week after our first meeting, we were going out. And slowly but surely, I discovered Azalea's many depths and heights: her favorite band (Sonic Youth), her favorite book (Perfume), her favorite song (Sonic Youth's 'Diamond Sea')… you know. Things you find out in dates. Small details that make the other person more human, more complex. Details that make them richer in your eyes.

Two weeks after we met, that's when we first slept together. The act was gentle, like a dance, every movement meant

to please and excite us both. For every kiss planted came a rain of quick pecks. The both of us were up all night. It was magical.

She fascinated me, her every movement, hell, her whole body and mind were like spells meant to enthrall me: how she raised her hand to brush away a stray strand of hair, how her chest moved when she sighed while asleep, how her bottom lip trembled when we kissed, how her tongue would touch her teeth when she said my name, how soft her lips were when we kissed, how she made little half-yelps when she came.

We were in the mall one day, and she saw this photo camera called a Zenith. She instantly fell in love with it, and she asked me to buy it for her, as she didn't have enough money with her. Of course, I was more than happy to pay for it. So, we bought it. We took pictures of everything and everyone: Mang Mario, the old man with the eyes of a Chihuahua tending his store; Cherry with her cousin-girlfriend Alex, in an erotic embrace; Mark playing chess by himself. We posted the photographs all over our room, in chronological order. Interesting thing about it was, you could see that Azalea's skill (she took most of the pictures) improved every two photographs taken.
"I'll be leaving for Canada in a week's time, Sid." Azalea told me one Saturday in the park. We were taking pictures of homeless people that week. Canada? For how long? I asked her, fanning one photograph in the air. "I'm not sure. I learned about it just last week. I've been trying to work up the courage to tell you…" she left the sentence unfinished.
