Dear Myra,


SCOOBY-DOO is spinning rapidly out of Saturn's orbit. We're down to 15% power, and according to the ship's AI, it's decreasing at a steady pace. I'm in Mess Hall, and with Life Systems throughout the station turned off, it is cold and dark where I am right now. I am running on my suit's back-up power, and it's down to 40% as of this moment. I'm using the Dicta-Scribe, so I have to be quick here, so I can send you this letter. Mi Ultimo Adios.

I get lonely here in Jovian Lagrange Point 7. I imagine this is how those seamen of old used to feel like, surrounded by water everywhere, floating idly by in their steel ships. That's chickenshit, actually, if you compare it to cosmonaut isolation up here in space. But it must've been hell during their time. I know it is, for me.

I have a photograph of the two of us in my left breast pocket. It's the one you took back in the library, about a week or so before I left. The one with you smiling in it. You have such an easy smile, you know that? Funny thing is, this photo is the only one I found with you smiling in it. You actually look a bit like an angel here, with natural light coming through the window behind your head, a golden ring-halo. Reminds me of a song: "'di magawang / alisin ang aking mata / sa anghel na nakikita / iniibig nga ba kita... ?"

You always were the one with the camera, snapping away at people and things like mad. I can actually count in one hand the number of times you turned the camera to yourself. And this is one of them. Like what I said earlier, I get lonely up here in space. My stay was supposed to be 3 months long. Pretty quick, when you think about it. But I just had to see you, even if in 2D only. I hope you don't mind.

With Artificial-Gee turned off, I can actually feel SCOOBY-DOO spinning in space. All the escape pods are jettisoning towards the nearest ISS, which is in Martian orbit. With the power at 6%, there's no way to stop the station from spinning off towards Andromeda.

And there's only one means of escape.

Years and years ago, NASA came up with a solution for times like this: a blue and yellow capsule filled with cyanide. The Space Program still issues out such capsules to their cosmonauts, just in case something like this happens.

I keep mine in my right breast pocket.

I miss seeing the sun rise, for some reason. Only saw that happen three, maybe four times in my lifetime, yet I miss seeing it. Terribly.

I remember first seeing it as a kid. We used to live pretty near the beach, and every morning, my dad would jog up and down the stretch of sand. Once, I asked him if I could jog with him. Dad agreed, so the next morning, I woke up pretty early, must've been four in the AM. Too early, it turned out, as I was out after ten minutes of being half-awake. I woke up at about fifteen minutes before six, and I looked out my window, and saw the sun rising. Orange and yellow and red and green. It was amazing. I loop that memory in my head every other day, so as not to forget it.

I miss seeing the sun rise. It must be the darkness of space.

My suit's back-up power is down to 18% right now. It takes 15% back-up to send a message to a terrestrial mailbox.

I am looking at our picture right now. I am making love to your sweet memory, right now.

I'm sorry I won't be home for Christmas this year. I hope you understand.

Pressing send after this. Time to blow this taco stand.

I love you.


- Juli Nadla
