I never had your fondness for things that have seen better days. I remember how you bit your trembling lip when workers razed our old rambling house on De Jesus Street to build townhouses of marble and Mactan stone. (Part of me was secretly glad they tore it apart before it would collapse all by itself.)

I can still remember how the floorboards creaked and the vermin scurried as we went on secret quests for secret doors and enchanted antique chests, charmed stuff that you were positive the house possessed. I had always equated age with squalor but you somehow managed to make me forget as you rambled on and on about new things to discover behind the wooden panels of Tia Sepa's altar with its creepy red incandescent light.

You, with your wide-eyed fascination, turned iron grilles with their rusty curlicues into a gateway to storybook worlds imagined. Like that time when we pretended it was Namia behind the old cabinet where you joyfully stumbled upon that box of old coins and those empty blue bottles that you said held magic potions that we could use to cure your dad. Even after the house was gone, you wanted to count the rings of those Balete tree stumps with their gnarly roots.

Whenever it was time to part, you took the sun and laughter home with you and I would lie in bed staring at gray drapes and droopy water-stained ceiling panels while deaf Tia Sepa yelled that it was suppertime and that I better go downstairs or starve in the dark.

I don't know exactly how many sunsets it took your mother to decide that it was improper for bubbly young girls to hang around growing boy's rooms. Eventually you moved away and I've since wondered how you fared as I struggled with the complications of growing up.

And so we meet again, in of all places, a hospital. You, in your white intern's garb, caring for elderly patients, me peddling boxes of diuretics and other German pharmaceutical wonders so I can surround myself with shiny new things that I hardly have time to enjoy. (If I keep going beyond my quota, next month I can replace my old sedan with a 4-wheel drive. )

Once again you regaled me with stories that surrounded you. Stories no longer imagined but all too real. Stories of patients whose names and faces, were it not for your concern, I would otherwise not have cared to know.

So I got to hear about Lolo Oben, the obsessive 76-year old schoolteacher with the most perfect set of teeth. You told me he had seven different-colored toothbrushes, one for each day of the week. I still chuckle as I imagine him pulling one out from a special ventilated drawer, and gazing lovingly at its bristles. You told me that Manang Mameng who dressed in rags and reeked of piss was a collector of money, a wealthy miser, who owned vast tracts of land. Then there was Nanay Auring who secretly hoarded all the hospital food brought to her, a habit she acquired in the Second World War. The list of the old folks and their strange collections goes on.

In a way, I'm the same as these people clinging on to tangibles, trying to make sense of what is left in their lives. What I want to collect are my few moments with you for you make me remember how it is to laugh like the children we once were. I'm grateful that you give me the time of day during your short coffee breaks in the hospital. I even cherish those few minutes of your talking in my car when you once let me drive you to a rambling old house, a different one from where we used to play, an ancestral home beautifully restored by the lucky man you call your husband. #
