1. Slipper Sounds

I can guess by the set of footsteps, whose feet exactly those are that I hear, going up or down the stairs in our ratty old apartment. Kuya's pudgy feet make this plick-plack sound as he trudges up and down the wooden steps in his smelly brown leather slippers. Manang Cita goes barefoot most of the time but her joints go creak-creaking and her heavy heels go blag every time she walks.

I like the sound of my own footsteps today. They sound different from yesterday.

Yesterday, like Cinderella, I lost a slipper. I lost my lovely right sandal with its banana-yellow strap and big black buckle. So where'd it go? Well, it seems to have turned into a lonely boat floating underneath these busy city streets.

Yesterday when I got down from the bus, there was this flattened tin can that I planned to kick all the way till it reached our doorstep. Don't ask me why, but when I saw this rectangular slit by the sidewalk, I aimed to shoot the can with a neat and swift kick. With a whoosh and a scrape, the can (and -aaaah!- my sandal) made their way into the gutter as easy as coins going through a piggy-bank slit.

Manang Cita is gonna kill me! I muttered and a huge lump formed in my throat. The sandals were new! We lined up a long time to pay for them one Sunday at the mall.

Maybe I'll just hop about on one foot, when I get in the house, and she won't see. I thought to myself, but I knew she would notice.

So when I got home I told her the truth.

Manang Cita looked the way I expected she would look and sounded the way I thought she would: "Ayyy naku!" She declared sternly. I walked up the stairs to my room with footsteps, heavier than hers. Much later, when I went down to eat, she clucked and went, "Tsk tsk, Next time don't go kicking tin cans on the street. You might get sick of tetanus!!!!"

Today I'm fine with my new slippers. They're red and light and made of rubber. I like the flip-flop sound they make every time I go up or down the stairs

My left sandal has not gone to waste. Manang Cita has made a hole in the cork and stuck a stick in it. She now uses it to catch pesky flies and cockroaches on the kitchen floor.

Whaaa-pack!

 

2. The Visitor

Kuya had this lady visitor at the apartment one really gray rainy afternoon after the rains. I was the one who opened the door since Kuya was still brushing his teeth.

Wow! (That was what I said in my head when she came into the room.) It wasn't like she was amazingly weird and had three arms or anything like that (and even if she had, it wouldn't really matter.) She was very beautiful, but not in a TV star sort of way.

She flowed into the room, like music. Little round brass bells tinkled from her wrists, which had lots more bracelets and charms and things.

She let me borrow the big beaded bracelet for a while. It was heavy. But her movements were so light, and her trinkets were like chimes moving with the wind.

While waiting for Kuya to come out and entertain her, she asked me how old I was and looked at the framed pictures on top of the piano. Then, she brought out these little drums, from this big basket that she brought and entertained me. The drums were made of stretched lizard skin which I found a bit scary and strange and fascinating. She taught me to make music on them with my palms. It was hard at first to get a steady beat. She just told me to close my eyes and follow the thump-thump of my heart.

Next thing I knew she was dancing (I couldn't keep my eyes closed for long.) She put on this weird sort of headdress. It was like a tiara made of beads -red, orange, green, blue, and crystalline- (not those plastic ones, sold on the overpass, mind you, but made of dark brown seeds, and yellow-green glass ) and feathers with flowers made of shells. Then she did this graceful flying motion that made her hands look like wings then she and whirled like she was starting a spinning contest with the ceiling fan.

Kuya soon emerged and took the drums away from me. (I didn't know he could play those things.) What a sight we were in the sala. The lady took my hand and we were whirling and twirling. Her flowing dress was like a flag, unfurling.

Like the TV says, "time really flies fast when you're having a blast". Before I knew it, Kuya soon had to bring her to the airport.

Later on, I found that lady's headdress-thing lying on the sofa. Manang Cita saw me and took it and put it away; way up in that shelf that holds all the thick books and other things shouldn't be played with. When Manang wasn't looking I took a stool, grabbed the headdress and ran to the roof deck.

I wore the beaded tiara and sashayed my way between the water tank and the TV antenna. There beneath the dripping clothes lines, I danced and pretended I was a beautiful queen, goddess of all the stray cats of Quezon City. The crown was too big for my head so I held it in my hand and spun around till I could see it make a nice blur of red, orange, green and blue. When I stopped spinning, I played make-believe that all the blaring bus horns on the street below were bellowing beasts, trying to blend in tune my own special music that they couldn't drown out. As I sat there catching my breath, everything in the city stopped being so gray.
