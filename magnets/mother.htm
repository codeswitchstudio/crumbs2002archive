When I was a wee kindergarten brat, I had two mistaken notions. First, I thought that all fire trucks in the Philippines were fire engine-red like in the books. Second, I thought all mothers painted .

At least that's what mama tells me.

She says she found out about this the very first time I went over to Caroline-Ruth's house to play with her pick-up sticks and these yellow Fisher Price blocks of assorted shapes that you'd have to correclty insert into the proper slots of a red and blue ball. ( For some reason, I vividly remember that part.) Anyway, when I got home, according to her (and this has escaped my memory entirely) I commented: "Caroline-Ruth's mom is a bad mom, she doesn't know how to make paintings."

Well, the tables had somewhat turned when my conservative great grandma inspected our house and passed judgment on mama who always let my sister Eggie and I "vandalize" the walls of our bedroom. (That activity was incidentally triggered by my sister who drew some stick-figure on the yellow-washed concrete wall.

That crayola graffti was followed by kiddie cave paintings; splatters and smears, trees and clouds and crescent moons in poster color reds, blues, yellows and greens. Eventually the two of us, filled the walls up. Whenever we had a fight we drew ugly faces of each other. My sister has the artist's hand. But I was the first one who learned how to write and spell "Eggie panget!"

When Lola Ling saw this, she said my mom was a bad mom. She didn't know how to discipline us yada yada yada. My mom, (those daubs of oil paint on her arms and clothes,) just smiled and went back to cleaning her palette knife.

Year later, I pick up that same palette knife and an old tube of hardened oil paint, from the dusty floor. In ma's studio, I move around some old furniture (to make room for my work station which I transferred from my bedroom where it's too hot to work in the afternoons.)

Days like this that I work at home, I still find comfort in the the faint smell of yellow Linseed oil and the sound of a well-worn paint brush swish-swish-swishing on spolotchy stretched canvas.

-Circe
