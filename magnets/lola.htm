My Lola's name was Gloria. She was a small woman, the old school sort. You know the type: housewife to an abusive army husband, spawned eight children (boys and girls), expert on baking leche flan... the old school sort.

We have this neighborhood store about ten paces from our place. An old lady tends the thing. And I'm telling you, she's old. Probably seventy-five, maybe even eighty-six years old. Her hair is cut short, a boy's hair-cut (my hair is actually longer than hers), white and wiry, like bijon noodles. Her pale skin is riddled with earth-colored liver spots (I imagine when she was younger, they were pimples), and her wrist's circumference is as thick as my three middle fingers combined.

That Lola is of the new school sort. The aggressive feminist grandmother, she hefts bottles of beer to and from their closet-freezer. She calculates a customer's purchase on her head (pretty fast at it, too). She even hauled out three bottles of those one-and-a-half liters sodas that I bought from her yesterday, all in one go.

And when she laughs, a sweet cackling laugh, music to your ears, her crow-feet lined eyes smiling with her.

My Lola died when she was sixty-seven. Doctors said it was heart failure (in the autopsy, they found out that her heart had sunk so low into her torso, they saw it about an inch or so above her stomach). I call it heart-break. Her last few months were spent on her bed in her room watching Spanish soap operas, a frown on her face half of the day, coughing like anything the other half of it.

The Lola spends her days tending her store, watching Tagalog-dubbed soap operas on her telly beside the shelf where the cigarettes are. She looks like she'll be ninety in two years, and I still hear her sweet cackle, when it's quiet enough in the neighborhood.
-Juli
