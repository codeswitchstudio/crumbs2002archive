It was a dark and stormy night.

"Where's your mother?" my father asked me. I just shrugged my shoulders in response. I didn't know where my mother was. "She left to buy some ensemadas from the bakery." I told him. That was about five minutes ago, and the bakery was along Leveriza, two streets from our place (we lived in this apartment, at the M. Santos extension).

The rain came pouring down three minutes earlier, and it was strong, coming down in buckets. The sort that you had to speak up to be heard above the rattle and jingle of our place's tin-plated roof, stamped on by rain. There was a storm coming, and if i remember correctly, its name was Daisy (our storms were given women's names). I was sitting with my legs crossed in front of me, playing with Snake-Eyes and StormShadow. StormShadow has Snake-Eyes trapped inside a Lady's Choice clear-glass jar full of water.

"What's taking her so long, then?" my father asked me again. Standing by the door, looking out to the rain, each flash of thunder and lightning coating his features with a cold blue sheet. "She must be waiting for the rain to stop." I answered, my eyes leaving the two ninjas only for a second.

A beat, before he said "Go fetch your mother." An order from on high.

Hesitating a bit, I stood from my spot, unfolding my legs like interlocking bridges. We had an umbrella somewhere in the house, but in mild anger from my father's request (what can you do, right? I was nine years old, then), I ran out of the house without one.

A brown river of rat piss and sewer muck was rushing along the street in front of me. The water came up to people's inner thighs, but they were adults, and were taller than I was back then. My clothes were now clinging to me like second skin, wet and transparent. Without much thought, I jumped into the flood.

The water came up to my navel. Under the lampposts' light, I saw people under bright umbrellas, and people outside them. I nodded and smiled at the ones without umbrellas, brothers and sisters for the night.

Amidst the drowning sound of rain, I could hear my teeth chattering. My spine and abdomen were rippling from the cold. I wrapped my arms around me, for warmth. The rain was cold. The night was cold. I felt warmth around my eyes, though, right before the tears rolled down my face, before they got lost in the raindrops, mixing and mingling with them. I closed my eyes and shivered in the rain. My testicles shrivelled into me.

"Sid, is that you?" I heard my mother tell me. I opened my eyes and there she was, umbrella in hand, a brown bag of ensemadas in the other (steaming in the night, even), both wrapped around her, keeping the cold at bay. "M-mo-mom... Dad's l-l-look-ing ffffor... yyyyouu..." I told her between sobs and shivers.

"Oh, poor darling..." she told me, unwrapping her arms to embrace me. It was warmer, there, in her embrace. And dryer, under the umbrella.

I stopped shivering.
