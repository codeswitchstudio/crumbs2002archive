"A blister on the paper." I noted, scraping off the bit of wax on the page with my fingernail.

"Candle-drip." She smiled sheepishly. "Half an hour into the brownout, we ran out of batteries and I just had to see what happened to Alyosha."

Pata always returned my best books in a sorry state: battered, dog-eared, with the spine cracked and the cover frayed, as if vermin had feasted on the sides. This never stopped me from letting her raid my shelves on a regular basis. I was always unable to resist telling her about the newest title I got from the bargain bins. Often I found myself shopping for books with her in mind.

Savoring the shared pleasure of a story's twists and turns, the two of us would talk about book heroes as if we knew them personally, the way people in class would gossip about who two-timed who last weekend.

A fast and furious yet thorough reader, Pata certainly sucked the marrow out of the printed words that moved her. Often she'd get me excited about rereading the books that I lent her, rediscovering passages that were lost on me the first time (digging up details that only struck me when she raved about them.) Sometimes whole paragraphs would be highlighted or page margins would have cryptic notes scribbled in pencil. I didn't mind, really. It's not the paper, but what's on it. I rationalized.

I remember how in school, Mrs. Luz would meticulously wrap the library tomes in the thickest plastic to protect them from the elements and grubby hands. That wasn't enough to make the books Patricia-proof. In our library, during that time, the most beat-up books were the best reads. Even the beautiful leather-bound edition of Invisible Cities (the one with the gold-gilt pages) was not spared Pat's wear and tear.

Always, she would return books with this penitent look on her face and the librarians were never hard on her. How could they deny Pat her books? She was a voracious reader, a brilliant writer, the outspoken star-scholar in my literary club.

Many years later, I found a thin volume of my very first published work, sitting on a shelf in her flat which was cluttered with stacks of well-worn books.

It was daunting to see my book in perfect condition. Good as new.

-Circe
