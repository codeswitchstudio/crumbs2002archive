Great Aunt Eva's altar was an institution and sometimes even a tourist spot in Bukal, the province where I spent several summers growing up.

God, those summers. I remember, how every night we had to pray the whole rosary. Not just a decade of it, mind you. We'd recite the entire bloody thing with appropriate litanies to all the names of Virgin Mary and Aunt Eva's top ten saints.

Like repeated chants of prayers and the wrenchingly bizarre wails of old folks singing the pasyon; all that Folk Catholicism just makes my skin crawl sometimes.

I recall how Mang Pepe, the deaf spiritista, who normally spoke in sign language, would have the voice of either a real 3- year old boy or a really good anime voice talent whenever he got into a trance. (HIMALA!!!!!) Aunt Eva claims that he was possessed by the Sto Nino then. And during those trances, the supposed Christ Child would address each of us by name, telling us to be good children as we knelt in the room, our knees all red from the floor wax.

Aunt Eva's altar which was in her bedroom, was this wooden cabinet that was covered by a table cloth which had yellow pompoms at the hem. The cloth was red, a red carpet for around 30 or so statues, standing in formation with their pure smiles, like pins in the bowling alley of bloody martyrdom.

Except for the replica of the Black Nazarene, the statues were mostly these Aryans with elaborate Spanish conquistador robes, skins of porcelain or alabaster, deep-set watery eyes of the shiniest glass, and long sweeping lashes with permanent mascara

The largest one, a centerpiece in his own glass booth, was St. Joseph the Worker. Dressed in green and brown robes, with a wire wig of curls, and the kindest face, he was my favorite guy statue.

My favorite girl statue was this carved bust of the young Virgin Mary. It was small and white and simple, set like a cameo with folded hands and a face which had this almost Lolita-like playfulness. It was easy to imagine her sticking her tongue out when no one was looking.

I also had much fascination for this blue-tinted "Jesus and the Agony in the Garden" picture frame with those holographic 3d-effect things you could stare at from different angles.

For some strange reason there was a pair of Buddhas in there, too. At the back of the Black Nazarene sat the jolly big fat colored piggy bank Buddha with his Boddhisatvas. Behind the Lady-of-the-Rosary-Holy-water-bottle-with-the-screw-top-crown was the brass version of Siddharta in Lotus position.

The rest of the icons gave me the creeps. The smell of flickering candles and the eerie glow of a red incandescent light bulb were no help either. All that made me think twice about helping myself to the bowls of hard candies, coins, and anting-antings that lay on the altar which, incidentally, was right across Aunt Eva and Uncle Edong's queen-sized bed.

On that bed I could feel these sets of eyes watching my every move as my young mind wandered.

As for my Great Aunt Eva and Uncle Edong, I guess they never did naughty things in bed anymore.

Then again...

-Circe
