When I was in high school, I had this girl classmate named Leslie. She was beautiful, I tell you. As tall as I was (back when all the girls only came up to my nose), her hair was, in a weird way, cut short, an apple-shaped cut, the color naturally auburn. The cut was weird because when her head's in the normal position, her hair only went down to her jawline. But when she stooped her head a bit, it would flow down smoothly, and it created this weird illusion of being longer than it really was.

She had a pretty deep voice, too. One day, she called me up at home, and my brother answered the call. After our conversation, my brother went up to me and asked if Leslie was a boy. Her voice was that deep. She also had this boyfriend named Romeo. He was older by two years, and was taller than me (funny enough, I only came up to his nose). He was Vietnamese.

About a week or so before school year's end, Leslie came up to me in the daily school assembly. It was 6:30 in the morning, then. She held my hand and placed it on her chest. A bit shocked, I looked at her, and there she was in front of me, nose to nose, and she smiled. We walked to our classroom, our hands clasped together.

We talked like anything, that day. Like we were lovers in the past, separated by extraordinary yet unavoidable circumstances, like a World War, or maybe even a Family Feud (for a moment, I guess I was her Romeo), and that we only had the whole day to catch up on things missed, before I go away again for the French trenches, or before her nanny discovers us in her bedroom. Everything under the sun, from suicidal tendencies to ex-lovers to our classmate Paul Ian. I whispered to her my darkest secrets, and she whispered to my ear hers.

And all day, I held her hand, and she, in turn, held mine.

3:46 in the afternoon, fourteen minutes before the bell rang, I walked her down to the school gate, to see her off to her school bus. And about ten paces in front of us, waiting for Leslie, was Romeo. Letting go of my hand, Leslie ran to him like they were lovers separated by extraordinary yet unavoidable circumstances. Maybe they were.

I looked at my hand, the one that I used to hold Leslie's. It was hot and red. A blushing palm.

I went home pretty drained. My sleep was dreamless. It was a Thursday. That was around eight years ago.

I bumped into her last year, in Quezon Avenue. "Boss, chicks?" the man in red asked me. "We have lots here, boss." he said, gesturing loudly to a group of girls behind him. And then I saw her. A pale and pretty face among a group of brown. She was still pretty. You could see that she aged a bit, and her hair was now black, India-ink black (she actually looks better with black hair), and it was still short, that weird apple-shaped haircut that she had when we were classmates. She was still pretty.

She didn't recognize me. She didn't even see me. I just gave her a brief glance, stayed on her a bit, trying to catch her eyes with mine. Unsuccessful. She was distracted by a flock of nocturnal pigeons, pecking away at the concrete floor of the parking lot.

That day I spent with Leslie, back in high school... it was a pretty sweet Thursday, I guess. I don't get too many sweet Thursdays. -Juli
