At the inn, there was no hot water available. We had to lug the icy water in a bucket from the tap in the laundry area right outside Dinky's room. To get to the tap, you had to go through a maze of clothes lines where Dinky's huge and damp underwear hung, flapping like sails in the biting wind.

Dinky was the only one who insisted on taking a bath every single morning and night after a hike. Even if it felt like zero degrees outside. Even if the bathroom stalls, with their doors made of rickety wood and galvanized iron sheets, had busted locks that wouldn't latch well. When the inn ran out of those little shampoo sachets, Dinky climbed uphill to get some at the Shamrock Yogurt House.

Dinky was my alarm clock during our stay at the inn. Every morning, I'd wake up to his Sado-masochistic screams as he poured the cold water down his head. (Aaaaaaaarrrrrrrrrrrrgh!!!!! Shit. It's mother-fucking ccccc-cold! Splash! Aaaaaaaaaargh!) It was great to begin our day early so we could make the most out of the few days left of our stay in the Northern mountains.

It was okay when we were the only ones staying on that wing. After a few days though, a couple of Belgian backpackers bunked in the room beside mine, right across the shower stalls. (Lovely couple, those two.)

One particularly freezing morning, I woke up to Dinky abruptly stopping in mid-scream. Finding it strange, I opened the door and took a peek out the hallway.

I could see the back of the Belgian girl, standing outside the shower stall. She held the door open and said something mighty unpleasant to Dinky who was standing in the stall naked. Obviously, she had no need for screaming wake-up calls.

Later, all throughout breakfast (mmmm, Shakshuka) at the Yogurt house, Dinky's face was red as a strawberry. A blush perhaps or maybe from the cold.

I'll tell you about the Belgian hikers some other day. -Circe
