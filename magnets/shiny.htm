I don't pick up too many habits from friends. It's usually them picking up from me: a few choice words here an there, a certain accent, a certain mock-laugh... But, every once in a while, there are some people whose habits I pick up. Some good, some not, I guess.

Everytime Saffron and I would ride the MRT, we'd walk down to the edge of the platform, to the front end to wait for the train. It's automatic for her, I guess. And I'd be the puppy, following her wherever she went. We'd stand beside the platform's rear-view mirror (permanently affixed to a pole near the edge of the station, right at the mouth), waiting for the train to come.

Everytime we would ride the thing, we'd be there at the front car.

10 o'clock in the morning, in the Quezon Ave. station. I came from school, fixed up several things for several people. I was travelling alone, went there via the backyard, so to speak, and decided to leave the place through the front door.

I walked to the station, and out of habit (which I picked up from the countless times Saffron and I rode the MRT), I walked to the front end of the platform and waited for the train.

While waiting, I decided to waste my time looking at myself at the rear-view mirror (narcissistic, I know). And I saw the strangest thing.

I saw a boy's face, of indefinite age.

Sad, watery eyes, must be from sleep deprivation. Hair's dishevelled like anything, like a tornado ran against him. Skin's covered by a thin film of oil, giving him this bronze unnatural shine. Lips a bit pink from the cold. A medium-sized goatee, pathetically distributed at about an inch or two of chin-space. A thin moustache covering the top of his upper-lip.

It was mine. But for some strange reason, it was vaguely familiar, like it wasn't me, like it was someone else. Like I was looking at someone completely separate, completely detached from me. Like my twin. Like it was someone who looked like me. Or maybe it was an older me, my face from five years in the future? Or maybe a face from childhood, a neighbour from the old village, perhaps? Or a long-lost relative from Scotland?

I raised my hand to waist-level. I wanted to shake my reflection's hand. And then the klaxons sounded. I turned away from the mirror, and I saw the train pulling over at my side of the station.

I looked back at the mirror before I stepped into the car, but the mirror was in an angle, and all I saw were the people at the cars behind ours, trying to get into the train.

The whole trip, I was scibbling away on a piece of paper, writing a love letter for Saffron. But somewhere in my head, I was thinking "I had a close-encounter with myself, there."

It was weird.

-Juli
